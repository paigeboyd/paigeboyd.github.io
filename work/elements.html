The Brief

Part of a project during my university degree, we were tasked with creating a website about Elements, which would be used as a study aid for children aged 9-12.

The website should centre around the general chemistry curriculum taught to children within this age range in primary schools in Northern Ireland.

Research

My first instinct was to research what children aged 9-12 are studying about elements in school. Remembering back to school myself, I recalled using the BBCÂ Bitesize website to revise topics and take quizzes, as it was based around the NI curriculum. This proved to be a really useful resource for this project as I got a feel for not only what children were learning, but the tone of voice of the content which would help me to tailor the content of my website to this age group.

I soon learned that children in this age group learned basic points about a handful of elements - they didn't focus on the entire periodic table, as this wasn't information that they'd be able to retain easily. This was when I decided to stick with five elements for my website; a variety of elements which were in everyday products they might use, and the most commonly known ones.

Having not studied the periodic table since junior high school, I used this as a chance to familiarise myself with some 'fast facts' about each of the elements I was going to include in my website. The five elements I chose are:

  • Boron
  • Carbon
  • Helium
  • Lithium
  • Oxygen

I knew it was crucial to this project to design a website which would capture the user's attention, and retain it throughout. Children aren't necessarily going to want to study for school, and they definitely won't want to read through a load of text to find what they need. Instead, they need short, snappy content which is interesting to look at and memorable.

User Persona

I took the information about the users provided to us within the brief and created a quick user persona. This was an important step to take because it's crucial that I consider the user in every stage of the research and design process, and doing this helped me understand the user better.

When creating the persona I was envisioning a family friend who would fall in to this age group. Whilst user personas should use images of real people, I just used an illustration in his place for this one, with icons representing what he was interested in.

Wireframing

With my content gathered and laid out in basic HTML files, I was able to take a look at what I would be designing around. I took the content-first approach here because it is the main focus of the website; the design will simply enhance the content.

I knew from the outset that I wanted to create an illustrative website that would catch the attention of the children using it, so when creating my wireframes I went in to more detail and sketched out ideas for the illustrations as well. Click on the images below to enlarge.

While sketching I decided to go with a flat, cartoon-like illustration style that would use bright, vibrant colours throughout. I came up with the idea of making each webpage look like a different part of a chemistry lab; the experiments, tool station with posters on the wall, and a whiteboard section.

I took these drawings to illustrator, where I added in bright colours and decided to give everything a thick purple outline to tie the illustrations together. I created the background, foreground and SVGs for the animations on one artboard, then separated the layers onto separate artboards ready to export for the web.

I placed the content into the appropriate pages, then went back and began adding the images in. I wanted to work with the content first as it is important in this website. I used CSS3 animations and transitions on buttons and SVG images to create the effect of flickering flames, bursting bubbles and active buttons.

Reflecting on this Project...

This project was a first for me in terms of creating a fully illustrative website, and I really enjoyed the process. I think I made good use of a vibrant colour palette, kept the illustrations consistent and the CSS animations add an extra interesting element to the homepage.

Designing for children aged 9-12 made me take a step back and carefully consider the content and how I would present it in an age appropriate way. Using a content-first approach really placed emphasis on the importance of having clear, easy to digest content within a website.

What can I improve on?

I think it would have been beneficial to interview some users within the target market before beginning the design process and gathering the content, instead of just relying on the tone of voice and style of the content on the websites I used for research. This could have provided me with a different perspective to take on this project, so I'll be sure to do this on similar projects in the future.
