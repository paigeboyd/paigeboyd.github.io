What is Signar?

Signar is an iOS app and Augmented Reality concept which visualises how immersive tech could improve communication between Deaf and Hearing children. It focuses on promoting inclusiveness and giving children the exposure to Sign Language which is lacking in curriculums in education today.

Breaking down the learning process into basic, digestible lessons, outlines appear within the user’s view and Signar uses AR technology to recognise if the user is doing the sign correctly. Get it right, earn a point. Get it wrong and you simply try again. Gamified learning practices have been implemented so kids want to continue learning BSL, and take this knowledge in to the real world.

Signar also provides users with characters which act as interpreters. 2D animated characters analyse the conversation and translate BSL to spoken English, and vice-versa, in real-time, breaking down the communication barrier which exists in today’s society.

Signar also recognises how parents play a role in keeping their kids’ learning progress on track, and so the app also features a password-protected area where they can do just that. Alongside seeing what lessons they’ve completed and when they were last active, parents can then make the decision which lessons to purchase next. Keeping the lesson store behind this password-protected area means no surprise bills from Apple!

Target Market

Signar is designed for children aged 6–16 in the UK. Signar is targeted towards parents with children within this age range, of all hearing levels. It is limited to the UK as this is where British Sign Language is most commonly used.

Whilst the app is designed for this age range, in reality all ages can benefit from the simplistic and gamified learning style.

Market Research

The apps I came across and reviewed on Tumblr are:

  • Sign BSL
  • BSL Hands One App
  • ASL Learning App
  • ASL Kids
  • Signly

I was quite disheartened to see a real lack in fun and engaging apps available for children. Most apps seem to be just the bare-bones, links to videos and that’s it, really. There’s no sense of education being fun or modern apps. With education increasingly incorporating technology into curriculums in schools, it seems that sign language education has been left behind in this aspect.

American Sign Language apps are appearing to be better than British Sign Language Apps in these areas, so it is this gap in the market I want to address.

The best app out of this bunch was really the ASL Kids app. This one was engaging and interesting to look at. It carried its branding throughout the app in the form of illustrations, used children’s voices for the sound clips and had children signing.

User Research

Last year I began to learn some BSL basic signs, starting off with how to spell my name, my age etc. During this time I felt like the resources out there weren’t designed the best and actually made the learning process quite difficult as attending a class wasn’t something that was available in my area. I turned to the App Store where I was shocked at how little there was available and I noticed that there were no apps for children.

I created a survey and sent it out via Facebook to people in my area about Sign Language education in schools and if their kids used educational apps. Augmented Reality Prototypes

When the time came to start designing the Augmented Reality part of the project, I decided to work with After Effects. To cut a long process, short; I created multiple video layers and inserted my UI and illustrations between them, then animated them to fit in with the user's gestures.

I wrote an article on my design process for these AR protoypes. You can head over to the ARVR Journey on Medium to read more about it!

This video demonstrates how the ‘Learn Sign Language’ part of the app works. Users put on their glasses and choose from a series of lessons. This example shows the Alphabet lesson. Users fit their hands into the outline they will see through the headset, then the AR technology will recognise if they are signing the word correctly. Correct will show a green outline, incorrect will be red and the outline will return on screen.

User Interface Design

I designed the UI using Sketch and Illustrator. I used Illustrator to create the illustrations and icons, then imported them into Sketch, where I implemented them into the interface. I thoroughly enjoyed designing the app UI, as designing for children is not only a challenge but having freedom to work with bright colours and animations is an enjoyable process.

Once I had each screen designed, I created a document that explained the screens and what the main function on each would be. This step wasn't necessary, but I wanted to ensure I had thought through each screen and was happy with how they all looked together. Tap an image to enlarge it.

I moved on to creating the interactive prototype on Proto.io.

This was a new prototyping tool to me, but what sold me on it was the ability to integrate videos into the prototype, without having to move to an external source such as YouTube, which would take away from the experience.

I picked the software up relatively quickly, as I was able to import all of my design elements from Sketch which saved me from having to recreate them again. Another special feature of Proto.io was how interactive I was able to make the app. Text fields can be typed in and the user can use the swipe gesture and the UI behaves exactly how expected. Yes, this is only a prototype, but I wanted it to feel as realistic as possible. I was also able to use a few lines of JavaScript to create a slider function which displayed the users name. These features just weren't available when using Sketch and Invision, and I feel the added interactivity really drills home what I'm trying to achieve with this app.

User Testing

I tested the wireframes and basic designs with a select group of people who responded to my user survey back in semester one. I used their feedback to inform how I developed the design of the screens further. These are a selection of points I received back:

I think it would be useful to put the log out button in the parents’ section to stop my kids accidentally hitting it all the time and me having to be around to log them back in.

The 'Sign of the Day’ feature is a big hit, all feedback on it was positive and parents pointed out it would be a good way for them to involve themselves by learning the sign with their children.

… nice balance between the kids and parents screens of the app, it feels familiar with the rest of the app but a bit more mature … I like the animations and the illustrations are great for my kids, they fit the age group well.

Based on this I made one major change, and that was moving the location of the log out button to the parent’s dashboard, meaning it was in a password protected area and therefore out of kids’ reach.

This involved changing the design to include a secondary navigation bar at the bottom of the app (you can see this design in the UI design images in the previous section).

When the app prototype was finished, I asked as many people as possible to test the usability of the app. I did this by asking them to perform an action, then watched how they went about doing it. I didn’t give them any clues or help because I wanted to get a true representation and see if there were places they fell into difficulty using it naturally. I was so relieved to find that out of the 10 people I asked, none of them had difficulty using the app!

The main feedback I got from this was that the onboarding screens and their animations were useful in telling users what to expect next and how to use the app.

Reflecting on this Project...

Overall I thoroughly enjoyed the process of designing Signar. It pushed me to challenge myself and learn new skills, particularly in 2D Animation and creating AR Mockups, but I'm glad to have these skills that I can carry forward into other projects.

I think designing this provides a unique solution to a very real problem many people face today, and it's peaked my interest in designing for social good and to solve communication issues. My favourite part of designing the app was seeing the prototype bring together the UI designs and the AR Mockups. I found Proto.io easy to grasp as the interface slightly reminded me of Sketch so the learning curve wasn't to steep.

What can I improve on?

The user testing phase taught me that I should have spoken to my users again before I had finished designing the UI. Maybe if I had presented the group with rough designs that outlined what I had envisioned, I possibly could have received some of the feedback earlier such as hiding the log out button in the parent's area. UX Design is user-driven, so keep talking to your users to find out what they need.
